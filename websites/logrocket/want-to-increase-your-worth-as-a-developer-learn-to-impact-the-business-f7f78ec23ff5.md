### Speak the¬†language

It‚Äôs often said that developers and the rest of the business speak different languages.

There‚Äôs some truth to that.

Developers, on a daily basis, tackle complex issues like scalability, reusability, clarity, calculated risks, and many more. Business primarily speaks in two dialects: time and money. Don‚Äôt get me wrong‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääthese things can be very complex and are absolutely vital, they are not simple things. There are different things that affect this, of course, like quality and relationships‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääbut to really get the attention of the business, time and money are what you want to talk about.

Now, I can already hear some of you saying: ‚ÄúWait a minute! Developers think of those things all the time!‚Äù

And that‚Äôs true too.

We think about sustainable architecture, reusable code, and effective third-party solutions quite often, and those translate to time and money. Just like almost anything you can say in English has a good Spanish translation, developers end up talking about time and money quite a bit, but we usually don‚Äôt deliver that translation. We don‚Äôt do it in language that the business can relate to.

#### Combating the dreaded rush¬†job

Let‚Äôs say a project team is demanding that you do a rush job on a new feature. It‚Äôs technically possible to get the feature done on their timeline, but the resulting code will be unwieldy: it won‚Äôt be reusable, it will make future updates slower, and it increases the likelihood of bugs on that page.

‚ÄúThat‚Äôs spaghetti code! We‚Äôre going to have to work around that code every time we touch the page, and that‚Äôs all wasted time if we need to do this again!‚Äù

All valid points, but it‚Äôs in the wrong language.

### Sell solutions, not technologies

#### Find problems before you build solutions

In the example above, the sale is not explicitly tied to a problem you have. It could be that the new oil _will_ make things much better for you, but how would you know? ### Efficiency isn‚Äôt just for¬†you

When I was first getting started in dev, I worked for a company that was extremely focused on cutting costs. Employees and managers were praised and sometimes promoted when they brought ideas to the table to reduce the time and money we had to spend to get things done.

This culture soon imploded and things got out of hand.

Teams were lauded for efficiency gains that mostly consisted of pushing your team‚Äôs work off onto another team. That team became overwhelmed and had to figure out how to push some of their work onto _another_ team. Upper management thought it was great how much ‚Äúinnovation‚Äù was going on, but it was killing the company.

There were two problems there. One, there was no camaraderie amongst the teams‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääeach team just looked out for its own, and inter-team conflicts sometimes got vicious. Two, there was no net gain‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääin fact, there was often a net loss, as teams had to adjust to getting new and unfamiliar work done and constantly-shifting responsibilities made things difficult to adjust to for employees.

Both problems illustrate the same point: efficiency needs to be measured across the whole company, not just your team.

#### Take a step back to spring three steps¬†forward

At another company a few years back, I worked out some significant gains for the creative department. They were making Photoshop comps for pages that Photoshop wasn‚Äôt really built for: contact forms, admin panels, that sort of thing.

> How long does it take you to build a good-looking form? Probably not very¬†long.

> How long does it take a designer to design one? Probably quite a bit¬†longer.

> How long does it take you to build completely custom form elements because the designer didn‚Äôt know which enhancements were simple and which were painstaking?

> Way longer than it¬†should.

When the designer got the shell of a website done and got to the contact form or admin panel, I would take over and take a stab at it. When I built something that looked pretty good, the designer would come over and we‚Äôd work together to tweak it. If the designer requested something small that would be a big change, they‚Äôd find out right there and usually changed their request to something easier. They even got some new ideas when I suggested simple CSS enhancements. I ended up spending a little more time than usual on the project, yes, but the overall timeline went way down, saving us and the client time and money.

Now, this only worked because both the designer and I were willing to sacrifice some time to help the other. I tried this on another project where the designer took a note from my old company and just wanted to unload all the unpleasant tasks onto me to save himself time. If that starts happening, stop‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääit‚Äôs not productive. But I‚Äôve found that most people from other teams are happy to collaborate if it means saving the project some time overall.

Thinking about how to save other teams time and money is good on a small-scale, but even better on a large-scale. If something works well on a project, ask yourself if it could scale to work on all projects. Developers aren‚Äôt usually in the business of defining processes, and it‚Äôs a shame because we‚Äôre excellent problem-solvers and offer a much different perspective on projects than project managers or upper management. Like sales, this doesn‚Äôt have to be something you do all the time‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääafter all, we‚Äôre developers first‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääbut if you notice the same problems popping up on every project and have a good idea to solve that, sharing that benefits everybody.

### The easiest way to get¬†there

These are some foundational principles that can get you started on impacting business as a front-end developer. There are many more, including subsets of each of these principles. What do you do, then? Do you memorize them all? Do you make flowcharts?

> I‚Äôve found that the best way to get good at these principles is to practice empathy, for both your clients and your coworkers.

Saving time and money is usually the biggest problem your coworkers and clients face; clients need partners who care about their problems and work with them to solve them; your coworkers may need your help and expertise on saving time and money in their own workflows. Each of these starts with just giving a damn about what they‚Äôre dealing with.

It‚Äôs true that some people are more naturally geared toward empathy than others, and that‚Äôs fine‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääbut just about everyone can choose to practice it a little more. And choosing to practice it more is often the quickest way to get better at solving business problems for your coworkers and clients. When someone brings a request to you, ask what they really want, and ask why they care. Having the answers to these questions puts you in a much better spot to make a huge impact on that project and the business overall.
